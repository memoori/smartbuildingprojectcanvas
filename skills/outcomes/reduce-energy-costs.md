## Overview

Energy costs typically represent 20–30% of a commercial building's operating expenses — the single largest controllable cost after personnel. Reducing energy consumption directly improves Net Operating Income (NOI), the metric most building owners care about most.

Smart building technology achieves energy savings through three mechanisms: eliminating waste (conditioning unoccupied spaces, running equipment at full capacity when partial load suffices), optimizing operations (sequencing equipment efficiently, using predictive control), and generating or storing energy (on-site solar, battery storage, demand response participation).

## Achievable Savings by Building Type

| Building Type | Achievable Savings | Primary Opportunity |
|---|---|---|
| Office | 18% | After-hours HVAC, demand-controlled ventilation, scheduling |
| Retail | 14% | Portfolio-wide standardization, lighting, refrigeration optimization |
| Hospital | 14% | Central plant optimization, non-clinical zone management |
| Hotel | 8% | Guest room HVAC based on occupancy, central plant |
| Data Center | Up to 40% | AI-driven cooling optimization (Google/Phaidra case) |

Source: ACEEE benchmarks and industry case studies.

## Investment Context

- **Typical ROI:** 3:1 over 5 years (JLL estimate for smart building tech broadly)
- **HVAC optimization payback:** 2–5 years
- **Sub-metering + analytics payback:** 1–3 years
- **EMIS cost:** $0.50–$3.00 per sq ft total
- **AI HVAC overlay:** $0.10–$0.50 per sq ft annually

However, IoT project success rates remain sobering: only 16% are fully realized (Beecham Research, 2023). The most common failure mode is installing technology without connecting it to operational decisions — dashboards that nobody acts on, sensors that collect data but don't trigger control changes.

## Technology Priorities for This Outcome

1. **Energy management / EMIS** — You can't reduce what you don't measure. Sub-metering and analytics are the foundation.
2. **HVAC optimization** — The biggest consumer is the biggest opportunity. Start with scheduling and setpoints, then advance to predictive/AI control.
3. **Occupancy sensing** — Demand-controlled operations based on real occupancy instead of assumed schedules.
4. **Lighting controls** — Occupancy-based dimming and daylight harvesting. 20–50% lighting energy savings achievable.
5. **BMS modernization** — A capable BMS is the prerequisite for all optimization. Legacy systems often can't execute modern control strategies.

## Canvas Section Guidance

### Section 1: Goals
Frame energy reduction as an NOI improvement, not a technology project. Quantify the opportunity: "Our building spends $X per sq ft on energy; comparable smart buildings achieve $Y — that's $Z annual savings."

### Section 4: Costs & Risk
The biggest risk is underinvestment in commissioning and ongoing optimization. Technology installed but not properly commissioned delivers 0% savings. Budget for commissioning, training, and at least 12 months of performance monitoring.

### Section 7: KPIs
- Energy Use Intensity (EUI): kBtu/sq ft/year
- ENERGY STAR score
- Energy cost per sq ft (normalize for weather and occupancy)
- Peak demand (kW)
- After-hours energy ratio
- Greenhouse gas emissions (metric tons CO2e)
