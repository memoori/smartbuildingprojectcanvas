## Overview

Office buildings represent the largest segment of smart building technology adoption: 33.7% of all IoT sensors deployed, with 650M+ devices in 2024 forecast to reach 1.3B by 2030 (12.3% CAGR). ACEEE research shows 18% energy savings are achievable through smart building strategies in offices.

The office sector is undergoing fundamental transformation driven by hybrid work. When average daily occupancy runs 40–60% of capacity, the business case for occupancy-responsive building operations is compelling — both for energy savings and for demonstrating to tenants that the building adapts to how they actually use it.

## Key Technology Priorities

1. **Occupancy sensing & space utilization** — Understanding which spaces are used, when, and by how many people. Drives both energy optimization and portfolio decisions (consolidation, subleasing underutilized floors).

2. **HVAC optimization** — Demand-controlled ventilation based on real occupancy, after-hours HVAC management, and predictive pre-conditioning. After-hours energy use is often 50%+ of total in offices.

3. **Tenant experience technology** — Mobile access, room/desk booking, indoor wayfinding, amenity access. 91% of tenants are willing to pay a premium for tech-enhanced workspaces.

4. **Energy management & sub-metering** — Tenant-level sub-metering for cost allocation, ENERGY STAR benchmarking, and portfolio-wide energy analytics.

5. **Access control modernization** — Mobile credentials, touchless entry, visitor management. Multi-tenant buildings need partitioned access with property management oversight.

## Unique Considerations

### Multi-Tenant Complexity
Most office buildings are multi-tenant, creating challenges around:
- **Cost allocation** — Fair distribution of shared energy costs
- **Access partitioning** — Independent tenant access domains with landlord oversight
- **Network segmentation** — Tenant data isolation on shared building infrastructure
- **Competing preferences** — Different tenants want different temperatures, lighting levels, access hours

### Class A vs. Class B/C
- **Class A** — Early adopters of smart building tech, using it to justify premium rents and attract tenants. SmartScore and WELL certifications as competitive differentiators.
- **Class B/C** — Technology as a way to compete with Class A. Often the stronger ROI case because the baseline is lower — even basic improvements (scheduling, setpoint optimization) yield significant savings.

### Hybrid Work Impact
- Occupancy is unpredictable day-to-day, making traditional scheduling inadequate
- Hotdesking and flexible space require real-time availability information
- Buildings must demonstrate value to attract workers back to the office
- Right-sizing the portfolio based on actual utilization data

## Common Use Cases for RFIs

- "We want to reduce energy costs by 15% without compromising tenant comfort"
- "We need to understand how our space is actually being used post-pandemic"
- "We want to offer a mobile-first tenant experience comparable to Class A competitors"
- "We need to achieve LEED Gold for our new development"
- "We want to right-size our HVAC operations for actual occupancy patterns"

## KPIs to Measure Success

- Energy use intensity (EUI) — kBtu per sq ft per year
- ENERGY STAR score (1–100)
- Tenant satisfaction score (survey-based)
- Space utilization rate (% of capacity used on average)
- After-hours energy ratio (after-hours consumption / total consumption)
- Maintenance response time
- Certification achieved (LEED, WELL, SmartScore)
