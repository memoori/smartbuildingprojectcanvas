## Overview

Mixed-use developments combine two or more functions — typically office, residential, retail, hotel, and/or entertainment — in a single building or connected campus. They represent the most complex technology requirements in commercial real estate because each function has different operational schedules, user expectations, environmental requirements, and regulatory frameworks.

The smart building challenge in mixed-use is integration without interference: a retail tenant's evening hours shouldn't heat the office floors above, a residential unit's 24/7 HVAC shouldn't be billed to the commercial tenant, and a hotel guest's mobile key shouldn't grant access to the office lobby.

## Key Technology Priorities

1. **Unified identity & access management** — A single identity system that grants appropriate access across functions: residents get 24/7 access to their floor + amenities; office workers get business-hours access; hotel guests get room + public area access; retail stays public-facing. The hardest challenge in mixed-use technology.

2. **Central plant optimization with fair allocation** — Shared chiller/boiler plants serving different functions with different load profiles. Sub-metering and allocation algorithms must fairly distribute energy costs. Residential expects flat monthly fees; commercial expects per-sqft billing; retail expects tenant-specific metering.

3. **Integrated building management** — A single BMS or orchestration layer must manage systems that serve the entire building (elevators, fire/life safety, parking, common area HVAC) while allowing function-specific control (individual apartment HVAC, office floor lighting).

4. **Parking management** — Shared parking with different allocation rules: resident reserved spaces, office hourly/monthly passes, retail validation, hotel valet. Integration with wayfinding, EV charging, and access control.

5. **Network infrastructure** — Separate network domains for each function with shared physical infrastructure. Residential expects consumer-grade Wi-Fi; commercial expects enterprise networking; common areas need guest access. IT/OT segmentation across all functions.

## Unique Considerations

### The Allocation Problem
The defining challenge of mixed-use is resource allocation:
- **Energy:** How to fairly split central plant costs between a restaurant that peaks at dinner, offices that peak at noon, and residential units that peak in the evening
- **Elevator dispatch:** Priority algorithms that serve hotel guests, residents, and office workers without unreasonable wait times for any group
- **Common areas:** Who pays for lobby HVAC, parking lighting, amenity space conditioning?

### Regulatory Complexity
Different functions may fall under different codes and regulations:
- Residential units: building code residential standards, local tenant protection laws
- Commercial office: commercial building codes, ADA compliance
- Retail: food service regulations (if restaurants), public assembly codes
- Hotel: hospitality regulations, fire code for transient occupancy

### Stakeholder Diversity
Mixed-use has the most complex stakeholder map:
- Developer/owner (overall asset value)
- Property management company (operations)
- Residential association/board
- Commercial tenants (multiple)
- Retail tenants (multiple)
- Hotel operator (if applicable)
- Parking operator
- Each has different technology expectations, budgets, and decision-making authority

### The MSI Question
Mixed-use developments are the strongest case for a Master Systems Integrator. With 5+ technology subsystems that must interoperate across functions, no single vendor covers the full scope. The MSI ensures coherent integration, data architecture, and lifecycle management.

## Common Use Cases for RFIs

- "We need a unified access system for our residential-office-retail development where each function has independent management but shares common areas"
- "We want to optimize our central plant across three different load profiles (office, residential, hotel) and allocate costs fairly"
- "We need integrated parking management for 500 spaces shared between residents, office workers, and retail customers"
- "We want a single building operations center that monitors all functions without giving any single tenant visibility into another's operations"
- "We need to achieve SmartScore certification for the commercial portion while meeting residential smart home expectations"

## KPIs to Measure Success

- Overall building EUI (benchmarked against mixed-use comparables)
- Energy cost allocation accuracy and tenant satisfaction with billing
- Cross-function access system uptime
- Elevator wait time by function (residential vs. office vs. hotel)
- Parking utilization by function and time of day
- Common area energy cost as % of total operating budget
- Tenant/resident satisfaction scores (separate surveys per function)
